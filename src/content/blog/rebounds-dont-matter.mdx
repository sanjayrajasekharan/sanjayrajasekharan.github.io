Every missed shot creates a possession battle. The defense wins about 69% of the
time — that's the baseline. An offensive rebound is an *extra possession* that
shouldn't have existed. But who deserves credit for that outcome?

Traditional basketball analytics answers simply: whoever grabbed the ball. A
player's defensive rebound percentage (DRB%) or offensive rebound percentage
(ORB%) is their "rebounding value." Simple, measurable, wrong.

## The problem with counting rebounds

Rebounds are a **team outcome** masquerading as an individual statistic. When
Rudy Gobert boxes out two opponents and the ball bounces to Mike Conley, Conley's
DRB% goes up. Gobert's doesn't. The box score credits the collector, not the
contributor.

This isn't a new observation — basketball people have known this for years. But
the usual response is hand-waving: "yeah, rebounds are noisy." We wanted to
quantify *how* noisy, and build something better.

## A possession-level model

We built a hierarchical Bayesian model that estimates each player's causal
impact on possession-level rebound outcomes. For every rebound opportunity in the
2025–26 NBA season (115,931 possessions across 1,230 games), we observe:

- The 5 defenders and 5 offensive players on the floor
- The shot type and distance
- Whether the defense or offense got the rebound

The model decomposes each player's effect into two latent factors — a shared
rebounding ability and a differential that captures whether a player is
relatively better on offense or defense. The key insight: credit is assigned
based on **who was on the floor**, not who caught the ball.

## Does it actually work?

We tested this directly. Four models, same logistic structure, same features —
the only difference is how they assign credit to players:

| Model | Player effect | What it represents |
|-------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Null** | None | Shot context only |
| **Traditional** | DRB% / ORB% | Personal collection rates |
| **Height-only** | Height z-score | Physical attributes |
| **Possession Impact** | Latent r + d | Bayesian — isolates individual from lineup |

Five-fold cross-validation by games. The traditional model recomputes rates from
training data only — no peeking.

## What this means

The magnitude is modest — shot context (type, distance) dominates any individual
player's effect. That's actually the point: **rebounding impact is a low-value
skill that traditional stats dramatically overvalue.**

When a team pays a premium for a "great rebounder," they're often paying for a
player who happens to be in the right place when uncontested rebounds fall. The
model suggests that the real value comes from players who shift the *probability*
of their team winning possession battles — regardless of who ultimately grabs
the ball.

## The possession framing

We call the individual metrics **Defensive Possession Impact (DPI)** and
**Offensive Possession Impact (OPI)**. The framing is deliberate:

- A defensive rebound is the *expected* outcome (69% baseline). It's worth 0
  extra possessions — it's what should happen.
- An offensive rebound steals an extra possession from the defense.
- DPI measures how much you *prevent* extra possessions leaking to the opponent.
- OPI measures how much you *create* extra possessions for your team.

Forget about rebounds. Only possessions matter.
